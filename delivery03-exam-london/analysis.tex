    \section{London Smart Meter --- Energy \& Weather
Analysis}\label{london-smart-meter-energy-weather-analysis}

\textbf{Household:} MAC000002\\
\textbf{Energy data:} Half-hourly kWh readings, Oct 2012 -- Feb 2014\\
\textbf{Weather (hourly):} Temperature, wind speed, humidity, visibility
--- Heathrow area\\
\textbf{Weather (daily):} Sunshine hours, precipitation, cloud cover,
snow depth --- Heathrow (Kaggle: emmanuelfwerr/london-weather-data)

\subsubsection{Objectives}\label{objectives}

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  Identify dependencies between weather parameters and electricity
  consumption
\item
  Engineer time-series features (lags, rolling averages, time-of-day
  indicators)
\item
  Test for stationarity and decompose the consumption series
\item
  Build AR, MA, and ARMA forecasting models with 48-hour horizon
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Section 6 --- Summary \&
Conclusions}\label{section-6-summary-conclusions}

\subsubsection{Key Findings}\label{key-findings}

\textbf{Weather--Consumption Dependencies} - \textbf{Temperature} shows
the strongest inverse correlation with consumption --- colder hours
consistently drive higher electricity use (likely heating). -
\textbf{Wind speed} shows a secondary positive correlation; cold wind
compounds heating demand. - \textbf{Sunshine hours} (daily) correlate
negatively with consumption: more daylight = less artificial lighting
and lower heating need. - \textbf{Precipitation} has a modest positive
link --- rainy, overcast days increase indoor energy use. -
Cross-correlation analysis shows temperature effects persist up to
\textbf{24--48 hours ahead}, validating the use of lagged features.

\textbf{Time Patterns} - Consumption peaks at \textbf{07:00--09:00}
(morning routine) and \textbf{17:00--21:00} (evening peak), visible in
the heatmap. - Weekday peaks are sharper; weekends show a flatter, more
sustained profile. - Winter months (Dec--Feb) consume significantly more
than summer months.

\textbf{Stationarity} - The ADF test rejects a unit root for the raw
hourly series (p \textless{} 0.05), so it is stationary in the ADF
sense. - 1st-order differencing is still useful to stabilise short-term
dynamics and is used for ACF/PACF-based order inspection.

\textbf{Decomposition} - Additive decomposition (period=24) on a 4-week
January subset isolates a clear diurnal seasonal component and residual
variation. - Because decomposition is shown on a January subset, it
should not be interpreted as full-year winter-vs-summer trend evidence
by itself.

\subsubsection{Limitations}\label{limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\tightlist
\item
  \textbf{Single household}: Results may not generalise to the full 5
  500-household dataset.
\item
  \textbf{Daily weather resolution}: Sunshine and precipitation are
  broadcast as constants across all hours of each day, ignoring
  intra-day variability.
\item
  \textbf{No exogenous variables in models}: ARIMAX/SARIMAX with
  temperature as a regressor would likely improve forecasts
  significantly.
\item
  \textbf{No seasonal ARIMA (SARIMA)}: A SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q,24) would
  explicitly model the daily cycle.
\end{itemize}

\end{itemize}
